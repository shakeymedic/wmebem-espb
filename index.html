An Infographic Summary: Erector Spinae Plane (ESP) Block for Traumatic Rib Fractures

The Clinical Question (PICO)

Population: Adult patients (N=19, median age 70) presenting to the Emergency Department with one or more traumatic rib fractures.

Intervention: Ultrasound-guided Erector Spinae Plane Block (ESPB) performed by resident physicians (non-specialists) under the supervision of experienced attending physicians.

Comparison: This was a prospective observational study, so the comparison was the patient's state before the block vs. after the block.

Outcome: Primary Outcomes: Feasibility, patient-reported pain scores (0-10), total opioid use (in Morphine Milligram Equivalents, MME/day), and pulmonary function (Forced Vital Capacity [FVC] and Maximum Inspiratory Pressure [MIP]).

Study Design

  • Prospective observational study.
  • Convenience sample.
  • Single tertiary care medical centre (USA).
  • Recruitment: May 2021 - Aug 2022.
  • No control group (before-and-after design).

Who Was Studied?

19 participants

Median age: 70 years, 74% male

Mean 4.5 rib fractures

A cohort with significant injury

Performed by Residents

6 residents (PGY-II or III) performed the blocks.

Erector Spinae Plane Block diagram and ultrasound image. (Used with permission of Mayo Foundation for Medical Education and Research, as cited in the paper).

Key Findings

100%

Technical Success Rate

Performed by residents with no complications, haematomas, or pneumothoraces.

7.0 ➔ 4.0

Pain Scores Reduced

Median pain score fell from 7.0 to 4.0 within 1 hour and remained low at 24 hours.

↓ 41%

Opioid Use Reduced

Median MME/day fell from 57.6 (pre-block) to 33.8 (remainder of stay).

Respiratory Mechanics

This is a key finding. The block didn't just help pain; it improved objective measures of respiratory function.

27.5 ➔ 60.0

Maximum Inspiratory Pressure (MIP)

Median MIP (cm H₂O) more than doubled from pre-block to 18-24h post-block. This change was statistically significant (p=0.016).

No Change

Forced Vital Capacity (FVC)

There was no statistically significant difference in FVC before and after the block (p=0.45).

Clinical Bottom Line

In this small, prospective feasibility study, the ultrasound-guided ESP block was safe and feasible for non-specialist Emergency Department physicians (with training and supervision) to perform. It provided effective analgesia, reduced opioid requirements, and significantly improved inspiratory muscle strength (MIP). While limited by its small size and lack of a control group, this paper provides strong evidence to support developing local ED pathways for this procedure as part of a multimodal analgesia strategy for rib fractures.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Highly relevant to EM: assesses feasibility by non-specialists (residents).
  • Prospective data collection.
  • Measures objective respiratory outcomes (MIP/FVC), not just subjective pain scores.
  • 100% success rate and no complications demonstrates a strong safety profile in this cohort.

Limitations

  • No control group. This is a before-and-after study. We cannot definitively attribute the improvements to the block alone. Time, placebo, and other analgesia may be confounding factors.
  • Very small sample size (N=19).
  • Convenience sample, which is high risk for selection bias (e.g., residents may have chosen 'easier' patients).
  • Single-centre study in the US, which may limit generalisability to UK practice.

FRCEM OSCE Style Questions

Examiner: "This was a prospective observational study with no control group. What is the main limitation of this design?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Identify the Design: "This is a 'before-and-after' study. It measures outcomes in a group of patients at baseline, applies an intervention, and then measures those same outcomes again."
  2. State the Main Limitation (Confounding): "The biggest limitation is the lack of a control group. This means we cannot be certain that the positive outcomes—like reduced pain and improved MIP—were caused directly by the ESP block."
  3. Explain Confounders: "The improvements could be due to confounding variables. For example, the natural course of the injury, the placebo effect of a procedure, or the cumulative effect of other multimodal analgesia (like paracetamol and opioids) that the patients were also receiving."
  4. Conclusion: "Therefore, this study can show an *association* between the ESP block and improved outcomes, but it cannot prove *causation*."

Examiner: "The study found a significant improvement in Maximum Inspiratory Pressure (MIP) but not in Forced Vital Capacity (FVC). What is the clinical relevance of this?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Define the Terms: "MIP measures the maximum *strength* of the inspiratory muscles—the ability to take a deep breath in. FVC measures the total *volume* of air one can forcibly exhale after a full inspiration."
  2. Explain the Finding: "Rib fracture pain causes 'splinting', where patients take shallow breaths to avoid pain. This leads to a weak inspiratory effort. The ESP block provides analgesia, which allows patients to overcome this splinting and use their inspiratory muscles effectively, hence the significant improvement in MIP."
  3. State the Clinical Relevance: "This improvement in inspiratory *strength* is clinically very important. A good inspiratory effort is crucial for deep breathing and coughing. This prevents atelectasis and helps clear secretions, which in turn reduces the risk of developing pneumonia—a major cause of morbidity and mortality in rib fractures."
  4. Address the FVC: "The lack of change in FVC might be because the sample size was too small to detect a difference, or perhaps inspiratory strength improves more rapidly than full expiratory volume."

Examiner: "How does this study, despite its limitations, apply to your practice in a UK Emergency Department?"

Answer Structure:

  1. Acknowledge Limitations: "While this is a small, single-centre study with no control group, its findings are highly relevant to my practice."
  2. State Key Takeaway (Feasibility): "The most important finding for EM is the demonstration of *feasibility and safety*. It shows that non-specialist proceduralists—in this case, residents, who are equivalent to our junior registrars—can be trained to perform this block successfully and with no complications."
  3. Link to Practice: "In the UK, we manage many elderly patients with rib fractures who are at high risk of complications from poor analgesia and opioids. This paper provides strong evidence that the ESP block is a safe and effective tool that could be added to our multimodal analgesia pathway."
  4. Conclusion: "Therefore, this study supports the development of a local training and governance pathway to credential ED clinicians to perform ESP blocks, as it could significantly improve our patients' respiratory outcomes and reduce their opioid requirements."
